\section{Structural Characterisations}
\label{sec:interpreting-paths}

In this section, we will be interested in extracting a very regular structure
from a graph class $\mathcal{C}$ that is not \kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered}.
This structure will take three forms, the first one is a combinatorial notion
of \kl{regular sequence}, in continuation with the work of \cite{LOPEZ24} and
our \cref{cor:hereditary-perfect-boughs-wqo}. The second one is a notion of
\kl{periodic sequence} that originates from \cite[Section 7]{ALM17}, and is at
the heart of their \cite[Conjecture 2]{ALM17} that we adapted to our setting in
\cref{lozin:conj}. Those structural notions encode ``path-like'' behaviour, and
we actually manage to extract from those all finite paths using an
\kl{existential interpretation}, answering positively to
\cref{conj:path-transduction}.

\begin{definition}
  A \intro{regular sequence (of graphs)} is given by a finite graph $G$,
  a labelling function $\lab \colon V(G) \to \Sigma$ for some finite set
  $\Sigma$, and two sets $C, F \subseteq \Sigma^2$. It defines
  an infinite set of graphs $\seqof{G^r}[r \geq 1]$ as follows:
  the vertex set of $G^r$ is $V(G) \times \set{1, \ldots, r}$, and there is an edge
  between $(u,i)$ and $(v,j)$ with $i \leq j$ if and only if one of the following holds:
  \begin{itemize}
      \item $(u,v) \in E(G)$ and $i = j$;
      \item $(\lab(u), \lab(v)) \in C$ and $|i - j| = 1$
      \item $(\lab(u), \lab(v)) \in F$ and $|i - j| > 1$.
  \end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
  \label{ex:regular-sequence}
  Let us consider the graph $G$ that is a path with two vertices, 
  respectively labelled $\bullet$ and $\circ$. Let us define
  $C \defined \set{(\circ,\circ)}$ and $F \defined \set{(\circ,\circ), (\bullet,\circ)}$,
  the graph $G^n$ is the \intro{split-permutation graph of size $n$} 
  that we write $S_n$ and can be seen in \cref{fig:split-permutation-n}.
\end{example}



\AP A \intro{regular antichain} is a \kl{regular sequence} that is an
\kl{antichain} for the \kl{labelled induced subgraph} relation when vertices of
$G^r$ are labelled by their color in $G$, and the first and last copies of $G$
in $G^r$ are additionally labelled by two distinct new labels $\triangleright$
and $\triangleleft$. It follows results from \cite{LOPEZ24} and our previous
\cref{sec:hereditary-classes} that such structures can be found in
non-\kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered} hereditary classes of graphs of
\kl{bounded clique-width}.

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lem:regular-antichain}
  Let $\Cls$ be a \kl{hereditary class} of graphs of \kl{bounded clique-width}
  that is not \kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered}. Then, it contains 
  a \kl{regular antichain}.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Using 
  \cref{cor:hereditary-perfect-boughs-wqo}, one can assume that $\Cls$
  has \kl{bounded linear clique-width}. It then follows from
  the analysis of \cite{LOPEZ24} that 
  $\Cls$ contains a \kl{periodic sequence} $\seqof{G^r}[r \geq 1]$.

\AP Our next goal is to show that can \kl{existentially transduce} all finite
paths from a \kl{regular antichain}. Remark that \kl{split-permutation graphs}
form a \kl{regular antichain}, but exclude $P_4$ as an induced subgraph. Before
that, let us briefly recall that \kl{existentially transducing} all paths is an
obstruction to being \kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered}.

\begin{lemma}[name={},restate=lem:transduction-not-wqo]
  \label{lem:transduction-not-wqo}
	Let $\Cls$ be a hereditary class that 
  \kl{existentially transduces} all paths, 
  then $\Cls$ is not \kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered}. 
  \proofref{lem:transduction-not-wqo}
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lem:paths-transduction}
  Let  $\Cls$ be a \kl{periodic sequence} forming an antichain defined as in \cref{lem:regular-antichain}. Then $\Cls$ \kl{existentially transduce}
  the class of all finite paths.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}

\AP Let $\Cls =  \seqof{G^r}[r \geq 1]$ and let $\lab \colon V(G) \to \Sigma$ be its labelling function with sets  $C, F \subseteq \Sigma^2$. 
Moreover assume that $\lab$ is injective up to duplicating labels from $\Sigma$, in particular $|\Sigma| = |V(G)|$. For $i\leq r$, we denote the $i$-th copy of $G$ in $G^r$ by $G_i$, and by $u_i \in G^r$ the $i$-th copy of $u\in V(G)$. We will proceed in two steps, we first define a formula producing a directed graph where there is a directed shortest path from $G_1$ to $G_r$ going through every $G_i$, but there may exist arcs going from $G_i$ to $G_j$ for $j\leq i$. We will then show how to handle theses backward arcs, and produce a path $P_{r-2}$.

\AP
Define the following formula:
$$\phi_{\rightarrow}(u,v) = \lnot ((u,v) \in E(G^r) \Leftrightarrow (l(u),l(v)) \in F) $$
Notice that this formula is quantifier-free. Informally, $\phi_{\rightarrow}(u,v)$ creates an arc $(u,v)$ if the edge relation between $u,v \in G^r$ differs if $u$ and $v$ are put far away form each other in this order (note that $u$ can be in a copy of $G$ that comes after the one of $v$ in $G^r$).
Let $H^r = \someInterp(G^r)$ for $\someInterp = (\top, \phi_{\rightarrow}, \top)$, note that $H$ is a directed graph as $\phi_{\rightarrow}$ is not symmetric. 
First, observe that in $H^r$ there is no arc between two non-consecutive copies.  

\begin{claim}\label{clm:nolongforwardarc}
	For $(u_i,v_j) \in E(H^r)$ only if $j\leq i+1$.
\end{claim}
\begin{claimproof}
	If $j \geq i+1$, then $uv \in E(G^r)$ if and only if $(l(u),l(v)) \in F$ by definition of $G^r$.
\end{claimproof}

From definition, it is clear that if there is a backward arc from the $i$-th copy to the $j$-th copy with $j<i$, then for every other previous copy, this arc also exist.

\begin{claim}\label{clm:regularbackward}
	If $(u_i,v_j) \in E(H^r)$ for $j<i$ then for every $j'\leq i$, $(u_i,v_{j'}) \in E(H^r)$.
\end{claim}

A spanning path of $H^r$ is a directed shortest path from $V(H_1)$ to $V(H_r)$ going through at least one vertex from each $V(H_i)$.

\begin{claim} \label{clm:spanningpath}
	There is a spanning path in $H^r$.
\end{claim}
\begin{claimproof}
	Let $V_i^+$ be the vertices of $H_i$ such that there is an arc from $H_{i-1}$ to them,  $V_i^-$ be the vertices of $H_i$ such that there is an arc from them to $H_{i+1}$, and $V_i^o$ be the remaining vertices of $V(H_i)$.	
	Suppose there is no spanning path in $H^r$, by \cref{clm:nolongforwardarc}, it have to be because either there is no path in $H_i$ from $V_i^+$ to $V_i^-$, or that they are empty (i.e. there is no arc from $H_i$ to $H_{i+1}$, in which case $V_i^o = V(H_ i)$).
	Let $C_i^+$ (resp. $C_i^-$) be the vertices of $H_i$ that can be reached from $V_i^+$ (resp. that can reach $V_i^-$),  and $C_i^o$ the remaining vertices of such that there is an arc from $H_{i-1}$ to them,  $V_i^-$ be the vertices of $H_i$.
	We now argue that if there is no spanning path, then one can embed $G^r$ into $G^{r+2}$. Fix $1<i<r$, and define the mapping $h$ as follows: map the first $i-1$ copies of $G$ in $G^r$ and $V_i^+$ and $V_i^o$ to the corresponding first $i$ copies in $G^{r+2}$; and to the $r-i$ $i+2$-th copy of $G$ in $G^{r+2}$; finally, $V_i^-$ is mapped to the $i+2$-th copy of $G$ in $G^{r+2}$, and the last copies of $G$ in $G^r$ are mapped to the last copies of $G$ in $G^{r+2}$.
	It is clear from construction that the $i+1$-th copy of $G$ in $G^r$ is left untouched by this mapping and that the first. 
	Suppose now that this is not a correct embedding, then it means that there is $u \in V_i^+ \cup V_i^o$ and $v\in V_i^-$ such that $(u,v) \in E(G^r)$ and $(h(u),h(v)) \notin E(G^{r+2})$ or the reverse. 
	However as $(h(u),h(v))$ is entirely defined by $(\lab(u),\lab(v))$ and $F$, it means that $\phi_{\rightarrow}(u,v)$ is satisfied and $(u,v)\in E(H^r)$.
	Hence if there is no spanning path, then $(G^r)_{r\geq 1}$ is not an antichain.
\end{claimproof}

  \AP
  Assuming $r > |V(G)|$, there is two vertices $u_i$ and $u_j$, $i<j$, in the spanning path of $H^r$, which are copies of the same vertex $u\in V(G)$. Take these two vertices to be such that the distance $d$ from $u_i$ to $u_j$ is the smallest possible in $H^r$. Let $t = j-i$, this is the period of $P$, i.e. the number of copies of $G$ it have to go through until it come back to a copy of the same vertex.   
  Let $Q = u^1,u^2,\dots, u^d, u_1$ be the vertices corresponding to the period. In particular, $Q$ induce a spanning path of $H^{t+1}$ (assuming $u^i$ is mapped to the correct copy) and $Q^r$ induce a spanning path of $H^{tr +1}$. 
  %furthermore assume that it is periodic, i.e. that $P$ go through the same sequence of vertices in each $H_i$, say $P_i =  u_i^1 \dots u_i^p$, and $P=P_1P_2\dots P_r $. 
  
  \AP
  If there is a backward arc $(u,v) \in E(H^r)$ along the spanning path with  $u \in P_i$ and $v\in P_j$ with $i>j$, then by \cref{clm:regularbackward} this arc exists between every copy of $u$ and $v$ where $v$ is an earlier copy than the one of $u$. 
  In particular this means that for every $u,v$ such that $v$ appears earlier on the spanning path, $\dist_{H^r}(u,v) \leq 2d$ where $d$ is the length of $Q$: $$\dist_{H^r}(u,v) \leq \dist_{H^r}(u,x) +1 +\dist_{H^r}(y,v),$$ where $(x,y) \in E(H^r)$ is a backward arc such that $x$ appear after $u$ and $y$ before $v$.
  
  \AP We are now ready to transduces paths. Consider $H^{2tr +1}$, let $u$ be the beginning of $Q$ and $c = \lab(u)$. To guarantee that the distance between two consecutive copies of $u$ in $Q^{2tr}$ is $2d$ we recolour every other copy of $u$ along $Q^{2tr}$ by a dummy colour $c'$. Furthermore recolour every copy of $u$ not in $Q^{2tr}$ by the same colour.
  Define now the formula $$\phi_{\operatorname{Path}}(u,v) = \dist_{H^r}(u,v)\leq 2d \land \dist_{H^r}(v,u)\leq 2d \land \ell(u) = \ell(v) = c.$$ Notice that this formula only uses existential quantifier (recall that $\phi_{\rightarrow}$ which is used to define $\dist_{H^r}$ is quantifier-free).
  This formula create a path on the vertices $u^1,u^3,u^5, \dots, u^{2tr+1} \in Q^{2tr} $, moreover this path is undirected as $\phi_P(u,v)$ is symmetric. The interpretation $\someInterp = (\top, \phi_{\operatorname{Path}}, \phi_{univ})$, where $\phi_{univ}(u) = l(u) = c$ (recall that only $u^1,u^3,u^5, \dots, u^{2tr+1}$ are the only vertices with colour $c$).
  In particular $\someInterp(G^{2tr +1}) = P_r$ where $P_r$ is the path on $r$ vertices, and the conclusion follows.  
\end{proof}
	
 The following corollary follows from \cref{lem:regular-antichain} and
 \cref{lem:paths-transduction}.


\begin{corollary}
  \label{cor:not-wqo-implies-transducing-paths}
  If a \kl{hereditary class}
  of graphs of \kl{bounded clique-width} is not \kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered},
  then it \kl{existentially transduces} the class of all finite paths.
\end{corollary}


\AP Finally we provide a last structural characterisation defined in
\cite[Section 7]{ALM17} that is not \kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered} similar
to periodic sequence of graphs. 

\begin{definition}[{\cite[Section 7]{ALM17}}]
  \label{def:periodic-sequence}
  A \intro{periodic sequence (of graphs)} is given by a finite word $w$
  over a finite alphabet $\Sigma$, and two sets $C, F \subseteq \Sigma^2$. It defines
  an infinite set of graphs $\seqof{G_{w^r}}[r \geq 1]$ as follows:
  the vertex set of $G_{w^r}$ is $\set{1, \ldots, r \cdot |w|}$, and there is an edge
  between $i \leq j$ if 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item $i + 1 = j$ and $((w^r)_i, (w^r)_j) \in C$,
    \item $i + 1 < j$ and $((w^r)_i, (w^r)_j) \in F$.
  \end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
  \label{ex:periodic-sequence}
  Let us consider the word $w = \circ \bullet$, and 
  $C \defined \set{(\circ,\bullet)}$ and $F \defined \set{(\circ,\circ), (\bullet,\circ)}$.
  The graph $G_{w^n}$ is again the \kl{split-permutation graph of size $n$}. 
\end{example}

\AP It is immediate to see that \kl{periodic sequences} are a special case of
\kl{regular sequences}, by grouping vertices of $w$ into a single graph $G$. A
\intro{periodic antichain} is a \kl{periodic sequence} that is an
\kl{antichain} for the \kl{labelled induced subgraph} relation when vertices of
$G_{w^r}$ are not labelled, except for the first and last vertices respectively
labelled by $\triangleright$ and $\triangleleft$.

While it is not immediate from the definitions that one can turn a \kl{regular
antichain} into a \kl{periodic antichain}, it follows from our proof of
\cref{lem:paths-transduction} that when we \kl{existentially transduce} all
paths from a \kl{regular antichain}, we actually isolate a \kl{periodic
antichain}. This answers positively to \cref{lozin:conj} in our setting.

\begin{corollary}
	\label{cor:periodicgraph_WPA}
	Let $\Cls$ be a hereditary class, then $\Cls$ contains a periodic sequence of graph forming an antichain if and only if it contains a weak periodic antichain.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
	Observe that the sequence of vertices obtained from spanning path \cref{clm:spanningpath} correspond  to a periodic antichain, with $\Sigma$ being the labels used on vertices of the path, and $F$ defined according to $G$.
\end{proof}


\begin{proofof}{thm:characterisations}[main]
  First,
  \cref{item:charac:bounded-lin-cw,item:charac:regular-antichain,item:charac:periodic-antichain}
  construct antichains, hence prove that $\Cls$ is not \kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered}. 
  Similarly, \cref{lem:transduction-not-wqo}
  shows that \cref{item:charac:transduces-paths} implies
  that $\Cls$ is not \kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered}.
   As a consequence, every item of the theorem implies 
  \cref{item:charac:not-2wqo} since 
  \kl{$\forall$-well-quasi-ordered} classes
  are exactly the 
  \kl{$2$-well-quasi-ordered} classes
  by \cref{main:corollary}.
    
  Conversely, \cref{item:charac:not-2wqo} implies
  \cref{item:charac:bounded-lin-cw} using \cref{cor:hereditary-perfect-boughs-wqo},
  \cref{item:charac:regular-antichain}
  by \cref{lem:regular-antichain},
  \cref{item:charac:periodic-antichain}
  leveraging \cref{cor:periodicgraph_WPA},
  and \cref{item:charac:transduces-paths}
  from \cref{cor:not-wqo-implies-transducing-paths}.
\end{proofof}
